### Option B - Comets

The other way to get that much water is to drop comets from the Oort cloud. Understand when I say "drop" I mean it quite literally. The entire Sol system is a big hill with Sol at the bottom and each of the planets as a divot. If you slow something out of its orbit it literally falls just as dropping a ball from your hand. Except the ground is the Sun and there's no friction. 

We will need to bring in sufficient number comets from beyond the orbit of the Kuiper Belt (Pluto's orbit) to Earth to all arrive in that 40 day window. Not raining torrential rain drops, raining comets. Adjusting "high" orbits is pretty easy as far as rocket science goes. The orbital velocity far away from the gravitation center is slow, little changes in an orbital velocity vector has a huge effect on orbital parameters. The average Oort cloud comet is estimated to weigh 4x10^12^ kg. I initially considered transferring Saturn's Rings (10^19^ kg), but there's not enough ice there. ### Heat of Vaporization/Condensation

All of that water vapor freezing to become rain releases heat into the atmosphere 40.7 kJ/mol when it becomes liquid and another 6 kJ/mol when it freezes. 6 kJ/mol is released when the ice is formed high in the atmosphere and re-absorbed when it melts to become a raindrop. Net zero, so we can ignore the 6 kJ/mol. That 40.7 kJ/mol should be accounted for. A mol of water is 18.0g, or 0.0180kg. A mol is the atomic mass of a substance in grams; in a mol there are 6.02x10^23^ molecules of the substance in question.

First accounting should be how much total energy is released per second by this process. 2.59 kg/sec/m^2^ would be 144 mol/sec/m^2^ for 5860 kJ/sec/m^2^. 1 Watt is a joule per second, 5860000 J/sec/m^2^, 5.86x10^6^ W/m^2^. The surface of Earth is 5.1x10^8^ km^2^, so 5.1x10^11^m^2^. 2.99x10^18^ W total. The Earth receives in the upper atmosphere about 1.75x10^17^ W of energy from the Sun total. We are at about 20 times that.

Enthalpy of fusion & vaporization a mechanism the universe uses to control the phase of substances as a bulk. It's a key part of equilibrium and keeping molecules "on the same page" about which phase they're mostly supposed to exist. It's an emergent property of energy conservation between colliding individual molecules. In this case so much heat is being released with nowhere to go that an equilibrium would be reached where no more rain can form. The heat of condensation would overwhelm the heat of vaporization and the water would go right back into vapor. No net energy released. This is why boiling-condensation-freezing are temperature and pressure dependent. If we ignore this and keep pumping in the mass of water necessary eventually the water will start condensing again per the water phase diagram (linked later). Water just keeps condensing because it must due to the pressure and the Earth would need to radiate off (via light) 20x the energy it is receiving from the sun to stay the same temperature. Caveat is as you increase temperature and pressure the enthalpy of vaporization changes, calculating this of off this hypothetical is not something I'm going to do because it's going to require calculus and if we're to this point it's details as everything multicellular is very dead and we're into bonkers exotic chemistry territory. [Enthalpy of Vaporization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization)

Using the Stefan-Boltzman black body law with .95 for emissivity (the average for Earth), we'll use the surface area of a sphere at 100km (well above the mesosphere, but below the ISS) for the emission surface 5.26x10^14^ m^2^... Which has the Earth glowing at 562.7°K, wavelength at peak spectral radiance 5150 nm which is deep in the infrared (red visible light starts at 700nm). Even breaking the rules it's not too crazy here. Though it should be noted that 562.7°K is 289.55°C is 553.19°F. Right now the upper atmosphere at that altitude is -95°C. I just so happened to have picked the cold spot to dump all of the energy from condensing the rain. This is serendipitous because we needed somewhere cold for the energy to go. Making this cold spot almost 400°C hotter is probably not good though. Higher in the atmosphere where the energy is received from the sun it gets 500°C-1000°C and that is transmitted downward via conduction. 

In these domains "temperature" is misleading because the number of molecules carrying the energy is very small. Remember this altitude is well above the ISS. You'd need a space suit. [Temperature is actually a measure of the average kinetic energy of the molecules in question](http://hyperphysics.phy-astr.gsu.edu/hbase/Kinetic/kintem.html). As they bounce off of each other they transmit some of their energy to whatever they hit, averaging it out, thus thermal conduction. "Space" isn't really good enough of an answer as to where to dump heat. Space is nothing, it's not cold, it's nothing. So you can only dissipate heat into space via electromagnetic radiation (photons). The way we're used to moving heat in our personal experience is by thermal contact, molecules touching each other. Conduction & Convection. All of this is actually a good thing because if space was able to be used as a cold sink as in our common experience, the Earth would be frozen and dead unable to retain its heat. The Earth is insulated thermally by space itself.
